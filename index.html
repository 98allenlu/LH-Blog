Storm of 1888

The aproximate location of Belden Shipyard in Hartford CT. Located where the Park and Connecticut River met, the area was referred to as Dutch Point.

The Allie H. Belden

Built on the Connecticut River in the Belden Shipyard in Hartford, CT, the Allie H. Belden measured 104 feet in length with a tonnage of 169 tons. Finishing completion in 1870, the schooner sailed across the Eastern coast of the United States throughout the 1870s and 80s. Her voyages were largely uneventful apart from one incident in the summer of 1887. The schooner struck the Nashawena Reef in the Vineyard Sound on August 18th, 1887, resulting in her sinking. Later, she was raised, repaired, and entered service again, albeit she continued to have problems with leaking.

Allie H. Belden’s Final Voyage

The story of Allie H. Belden collided with Lewes' history in March of 1888. On March 7th, the Allie H. Belden left East Boothbay, Maine, bound for Easton, Maryland. Partway through her journey on March 11th, the schooner, laden with a cargo of ice, sought refuge behind the Lewes breakwater due to deteriorating weather.

Throughout the day of March 11th, the weather in Lewes Harbor was rough but not desperate for the some 50 other vessels which were there. Wind blew to the southwest with showers of rain, although none of this was to compare with what was to occur. 

At around 11 pm on Sunday, March 11th, 1888, the wind suddenly changed and began to blow from the northwest with a “hurricane force” of 90 miles per hour. The storm, later referred to as “The Great White Hurricane of 1888,” had begun.

The Storm

During the early morning hours of March 12th, 1888, the 50 vessels anchored behind the breakwater experienced horrific conditions. The sea tossed and threw ships effortlessly while sleet and frozen rain hammered their crews. Capt Hardy Holt of the steam tug George G. Simpson later recounted that the waves were “running as high as a three-story house.”

As the storm continued, the situation aboard the Allie H. Belden deteriorated. Her anchors dragged, and she went aground a few hundred yards from the shore. In the face of the storm's torrential conditions, the crew unsuccessfully attempted to launch the ship's boat. The ship continued to be battered by the storm, and the crew resorted to climbing up the rigging of the mainmast for some semblance of safety from the waves, which by that point cleanly swept over the schooner’s deck. A newspaper later wrote that “the waves did not simply break over the schooner; they leaped over her masthead at times.”

The Lewes Life-Saving Service's Finest Hour

Founded in 1871, the purpose of the United States Life-Saving Service (USLSS) was to rescue the crews of shipwrecked vessels. It could be extremely dangerous work for the crews of the life-saving station, who became known as surfmen. The Lewes Life Saving Station was established later in early 1884. In 1915, the USLSS was absorbed into the United States Coast Guard. 


On shore in Lewes, the men of the United States Life-Saving Station awoke on the morning of March 12th 1888, to a scene of complete chaos. As far as they could see, stranded vessels and debris littered the beach. At around 5 AM, the surfmen, led by Keeper of the station John A. Clampitt, left their station in order to rescue the crews of the stricken vessels. Their attempt was prevented however, by the extreme conditions of the storm. Wind blew frozen sleet and sand so hard that it cut and scratched the men's faces, forcing them to crawl on their hands and knees back to the Life Saving Station in order to wait for the weather to calm. 


Approximately 30 minutes after their first attempt, weather conditions dulled enough to allow the Lifesaving personnel to begin rescue operations. Their first task was the rescue of a schooner about 400 yards from the beach which was in immediate danger of breaking up. With the crew visibly clinging onto the rigging of the vessel, the surfmen began rescue operations on the Allie H. Belden.


The surfmen began with attempts to secure a line to the Allie H. Belden. A line allowed the use of a breeches buoy, which, as one of the main forms of rescue for the USLSS, could carry people off of a ship as it was pulled ashore. The mortar cart, used to launch and secure a rescue line between shore and ship, was hauled into position on the beach. The first shot of the Lyle gun launched the line on top of the mast stay; however, the captain of the stricken schooner was unable to hold onto it. With the first shot failing to secure the crucial line, the rope was hauled out of the water, and the cart reloaded. Subsequent shots similarly failed to secure a line between the men on the shore and the ship. It was now apparent that the only way to save the crew of the Allie H. Belden was via the use of the surfboat.


Men of the Life Saving Service, alongside several pilots who had volunteered to help, began seemingly futile attempts to reach the Allie H. Belden using boats. Conditions aboard the schooner worsened, and by this time, a crowd of spectators gathered along the beach. They watched helplessly as all of a sudden the main mast of the schooner, with the ship's crew still desperately clinging to it, began to collapse into the sea. Fortunately, before the mast totally gave way, the crew was able to climb their way to the foremast of the ship via a spring brace that connected the two. Now on the foremast, they continued to cling on as the storm and rescue efforts continued.


Some time later, the surfmen and crowd watched on in horror as one of the crew, after hours of clinging to the rigging for dear life, succumbed to exhaustion and fell into the sea below, drowning him.


By 2:30 pm, the tide lowered, allowing the Life Saving Service to more easily reach the Allie H. Belden. Even with the lower tide, the Surfmen still fought hard, resorting to dropping their anchor every few feet in order not to lose their progress and be able to catch their breath and recuperate some strength. After 9 hours of rescue efforts, 5 separate attempts to row out to the vessel and three cases of surfmen with severe frostbite the rescuers managed to reach the ship.


Tragically, 2 crewmen of the Allie H. Belden were killed before rescue reached the ship. The surviving crew consisted of the captain, mate, and two seamen, who were taken from the rigging and brought aboard the Life Saving boat by the surfmen. All were exhausted and frostbitten but miraculously alive after the nearly 12-hour battle in the rigging against the storm.

Aftermath Of The Rescue

Upon reaching the shore, immediate aid was given to the crew. The captain and one seaman were in such bad, frostbitten condition that they remained recovering in the Lewes Life Saving Station for four days under the care of Dr. Hall of Lewes. 


The next day, Tuesday, March 12th, all that was left to be seen of the Allie H. Belden was the ship's timbers, some several hundred yards off the shore. The power of the surf and storm had completely demolished the schooner. Her cargo of ice and debris from the ship littered the beach. 


Although the crew of the Allie H. Belden was safe, many more vessels remained stranded across the coast. For days following the storm, the Surfmen of the Life Saving Stations from Lewes, Rehoboth, and Cape Henlopen continued to rescue the crews of stranded vessels. In all, over 150 lives were saved due to the efforts of the Surfmen, along with the help of pilots and citizens from Lewes.

Written by Historic Lewes Intern Liam Trageser, Summer 2025
